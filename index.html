Rare Earth

Rare earth magnets are strong permanent magnets made from alloys of rare earth elements. Developed in the 1970s and 1980s, these are the strongest type of permanent magnets made, producing significantly stronger magnetic fields than other types such as ferrite or alnico magnets. Rare earth magnets are usually plated or coated to protect them from breaking, chipping, or crumbling into powder.

Ferrite

Ferrite magnets are also known as ceramic magnets because of their low production cost. They are the most popular for everyday applications. Ferrite magnets are extremely popular due to their characteristics and are corrosion free. For long term performance they are superb; if looked after they are capable of exceeding most products lifecycles. Ferrite magnets can be used up to +250 degrees C (and in some cases up to +300 deg C)

Neodynium Rubberized Gripper Magnets (Flexible)

These threaded magnetic assemblies are made with the most powerful magnet material available. Each assembly features a neodynium magnet affixed to a flat steel disc and coated with a black protective rubber to prevent surface scratches. It is widely used for mounting cameras, car mounts, tracking devices, holding up revolvers and magazines

Magnetic Safety

  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) Safety: MRI machines use strong magnetic fields to create detailed images of the body's internal structures. Safety protocols are crucial to ensure that patients and medical staff are not harmed by the strong magnetic forces. This includes screening patients for metal objects that can become projectiles in the magnetic field, as well as ensuring that medical devices and implants are MRI-compatible.
  • Electromagnetic Radiation: Strong magnetic fields can generate electromagnetic radiation, which may interfere with electronic devices, medical implants, or even cause potential health risks in high-exposure scenarios. It's important to assess and control these risks in environments where strong magnetic fields are present, such as near powerful industrial magnets or MRI machines.
  • Occupational Exposure: Workers in industries that use powerful magnets or deal with electrical equipment need to be aware of potential magnetic safety hazards. This includes knowing the safe distance to keep from strong magnets to avoid accidents and health risks.
  • Public Safety: In public spaces, such as train stations or airports, safety measures should be in place to ensure that personal electronic devices, credit cards, and other sensitive items are not affected by strong magnetic fields that might be present for security purposes.
  • Magnetic Shielding: In situations where sensitive equipment or devices need to be protected from external magnetic fields, shielding materials can be used to block or reduce the magnetic influence.
  • Education and Training: Proper education and training for those who work with or around strong magnetic fields are essential to ensure they understand the potential risks and know how to take appropriate safety measures.
  • Child Safety: Small children and pets can accidentally ingest small magnetic objects, which can lead to serious health issues. Magnets with a high risk of being ingested, like those in some children's toys, are subject to safety regulations to prevent these incidents.

    • It's important to note that the level of risk associated with magnetic fields depends on their strength and proximity to individuals or sensitive equipment. Safety guidelines and regulations can vary by industry and application, so it's essential to follow specific guidelines and recommendations relevant to your situation.
